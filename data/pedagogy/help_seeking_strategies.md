# Help-Seeking Strategies

Knowing when and how to ask for help is a learnable academic skill. Research
distinguishes *adaptive* help-seeking — aimed at understanding — from *avoidant*
behaviour, where struggling students withdraw exactly when support would help most.

## Ask the right kind of question

- **Instrumental, not executive:** ask for hints and explanations that let you
  finish the work yourself, rather than for the answer. This builds competence.
- **Be specific:** "I get steps 1–3 of this derivation but lose the sign in step 4"
  gets a far more useful reply than "I don't understand this."

## Use the available channels early

- Course **forums** let you learn from questions others have already asked; search
  before posting, then contribute your own.
- **Office hours and tutoring** are most valuable *before* a deadline, not after a
  failed submission. Reaching out early is a sign of strong self-regulation, not
  weakness.
- A small **study group** distributes the cognitive load and exposes you to
  alternative explanations.

## For at-risk and disengaged students

Withdrawal is the most dangerous response to falling behind. A single message to
an instructor or peer can break the spiral. Make help-seeking routine: schedule a
weekly check-in so reaching out never depends on momentary courage.
