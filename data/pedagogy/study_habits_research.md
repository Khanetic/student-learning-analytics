# What Research Says About Effective Study Habits

Decades of cognitive-science research converge on a handful of techniques that
reliably improve long-term learning — and several popular ones that do not.

## High-utility techniques

- **Practice testing (active recall):** retrieving information from memory, e.g.
  through self-quizzing, strengthens retention far more than re-reading. This is
  the single most robust finding in the literature.
- **Distributed practice (spacing):** spreading study across days beats massing it
  into one session. The same total time yields markedly better recall.
- **Interleaving:** mixing related problem types within a session improves the
  ability to choose the right strategy, even though it feels harder than blocking.

## Low-utility but popular techniques

- **Re-reading** and **highlighting** feel productive but produce weak, short-lived
  gains. They create an illusion of fluency without durable memory.
- **Cramming** can pass tomorrow's quiz yet leaves little a week later.

## Putting it together

A strong weekly routine combines all three high-utility methods: short spaced
sessions, self-testing instead of re-reading, and interleaved practice problems.
Students who switch from passive review to active recall often see their quiz
trend turn upward within two to three weeks.
